## What is Net Neutrality?

> Net neutrality is the concept that Internet Service Providers (ISP) like Jio and Airtel should treat all content passing through their cables equally. That means they shouldn't be able to accelerate some data into "fast lanes" while discriminating against other material. 
  
*This topic is subjective and keep in mind that we have a lot of stakeholders involved.*

### No compensation for ISPs
  
These days, services like Google Duo & Skype are offering real-time video calls. Hotstar, Hulu offer high-definition video streaming. These services use vast amounts of data and bandwidth for free on ISPs infrastructure.
Infrastructure can be improved by raising prices. Many businesses use large amounts of bandwidth. Besides, without Net Neutrality, ISPs can charge more for their data consumption. While this will increase consumer costs in the short terms as the big players pass it on, it will mean better and cheaper access in the long term. At least, that's what the ISPs are arguing.
  

### We take the bad content with good
  
An uncontrolled Internet is an uncensored Internet. These days, anything can be published on the Internet under the guise of “freedom of speech”. 

With Net Neutrality, anyone can access content such as nudity, riots, suicides or other illegal websites. While there are plenty of safety vendors who allow parents to restrict the sites available on a family computer, more children have smartphones and connected devices with which they can get online without adult supervision. If an ISP could block these services at a network-wide level, this would go a long way to solving this issue. ISPs can ensure that the Internet is less obscene.

### We can control Piracy
  
ISPs can also crackdown on peer-to-peer file-sharing, which is responsible for a lot of illegal downloads, thus preventing piracy.
Also, the issue of illegal file streaming can be countered.
As a result, ISPs won’t have to pay more for illegal streaming.
Instead, the money which went towards paying the data for illegal streaming can be invested in building better infrastructure, which in turn improves product offerings to customers.

### Parts of the Internet could be free
  
If we consider the huge bandwidth services for what they’re worth, they might have so much extra money that “basic” internet (e.g. Wikipedia and Facebook) could be completely free.
Free Basics, an app that provides free access to specific services in parts of world, was already banned in India by TRAI (Telecom Regulatory Authority of India) last year for violating Net Neutrality rules.

### Net Neutrality slows things down
  
Net neutrality is a form of regulation, and that does not bode well for fast reactions to changes in internet technology and use cases. So while it may help small innovators stay alive longer, it might prevent internet-specific innovations from getting to us sooner.

### Net Neutrality harms the "Economy"
  
By repealing Net Neutrality, different pricing schemes could stimulate more competition within companies, which could be advantageous to the economy.
Because some people use the internet more than others, revoking Net Neutrality could allow companies who need more high-speed internet pay for it, while households who don’t could pay less.

### Net Neutrality is an Unnecessary Regulation
  
Today, while we have been led to think that net neutrality is essential.
Furthermore, Net Neutrality is said to preserve the Internet.
However, the opposite might be true. In fact, Net Neutrality may not be necessary. In all the years before, the Internet functioned very well without it. Additionally, the government’s efforts to regulate the Internet has always been counterproductive.
This success of the Internet proves that new regulations are unnecessary.
Moreover, the fears that consumers have are hypothetical.
Precisely, this is about ISPs lying to consumers or degrading content.
Besides, Comcast (an american telecommunications corporation) blocked Bittorent’s traffic eight years ago.
Also, Apple introduced Facetime over wireless networks before cellular networks.

  
  


*With such broken Net Neutrality regulations, the internet speed we have in 10 years from now would be the same as it is today.* 

*So, ask yourselves.*

*Are you willing to deal with slow growth simply because you refuse to pay more?*
